USCIS Adjustment of Status
Policy Change: What You Need to Know

USCIS issued a new policy memo on May 22, 2026 expanding officer discretion in I-485 adjudications. Here is a clear-eyed look at what changed — and what the headlines got wrong.

Immigration Alert Published May 25, 2026 · H&H Law
⚠ Attorney Review Required: This content is pending approval by Austin Kim, J.D. before publication. Flag sections marked [VERIFY] for legal review.

If you have a pending I-485 or are considering filing one: do not withdraw your application and do not make any changes to your case without first consulting an immigration attorney. The new policy memo affects how officers evaluate applications but does not automatically disqualify anyone.

What Happened

On May 22, 2026, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) issued Policy Memorandum PM-602-0199, directing immigration officers to apply broader discretionary judgment when adjudicating applications to adjust status (Form I-485) — the formal process by which a person present in the United States applies to become a lawful permanent resident (green card holder) without leaving the country.

The memo instructs officers to look beyond mere technical eligibility and to consider the applicant's full immigration history, conduct, and any adverse factors when deciding whether to grant the benefit. This represents a meaningful shift from prior guidance, which treated adjustment as largely ministerial once eligibility requirements were met.

The USCIS press release accompanying the memo generated alarming headlines in the immigration community. Many news outlets reported that applicants would now need to demonstrate "extraordinary circumstances" for approval. That language was in the press release — not in the text of PM-602-0199 itself. The memo does expand officer discretion materially, but the "extraordinary circumstances" framing overstated what the document actually requires.

What the Memo Actually Says

PM-602-0199 reaffirms that adjustment of status is a discretionary benefit — something courts have recognized for decades — and directs officers to apply that discretion in a more active, case-specific way. Officers are instructed to consider factors that may weigh against approval, including:

  • Prior immigration violations (overstays, unauthorized employment, prior removal orders)
  • Criminal history, including arrests, charges, or convictions — even minor ones
  • Misrepresentations or fraud in prior immigration filings
  • Conduct that reflects poorly on the applicant's character or regard for U.S. law
  • National security or public safety concerns

The memo does not create a new evidentiary standard requiring applicants to prove extraordinary circumstances. It directs officers to exercise judgment — which means cases with no adverse factors should proceed normally, while cases with complicating history now face a more formal discretionary evaluation.

There is no grandfathering provision. The new discretionary standard applies to pending I-485 applications, not only cases filed after the memo's issuance date.

Who Is Most Affected

The practical impact of this policy memo varies significantly depending on the applicant's visa category and immigration history. The chart below summarizes the risk level for common categories:

Higher Scrutiny — Consult Attorney Now

  • F-1 students with past status gaps or OPT violations
  • Applicants with prior overstay periods
  • Anyone with an arrest record or removal history
  • Employment-based cases with gaps in authorized status
  • Anyone with a prior denied application or appeal

Moderate — Review Recommended

  • Employment-based applicants (EB-1, EB-2, EB-3) with clean records
  • Family-preference categories (F-2A through F-4)
  • Diversity Visa lottery winners
  • Applicants with long pendency who had multiple visa changes

Lower Risk — But Confirm Your Case

  • Spouses of U.S. citizens (IR-1) with clean records
  • Minor children of U.S. citizens (IR-2)
  • Parents of U.S. citizens (IR-5)
  • VAWA self-petitioners

Even for lower-risk categories, this is not a moment to be passive. If you have a pending I-485 and any complications in your immigration history, an attorney should review your file before your interview date.

What to Do Now

If you have a pending I-485:

  • Do not withdraw your application without legal advice. Withdrawal forfeits your place in line and any underlying priority date.
  • Do not make changes to your case — employment changes, address changes, or RFE responses — without consulting your attorney.
  • Review your full immigration history with an attorney before your next interview or any USCIS contact.
  • If you have anything in your history that concerns you — a status gap, a prior denial, any contact with law enforcement — disclose it to your attorney now, not after USCIS raises it.

If you have not yet filed an I-485:

  • Get a case assessment before filing. The new discretionary standard makes pre-filing review more important than ever. An experienced attorney can identify risk factors and develop a strategy to present your case favorably.
  • Do not delay unnecessarily. A pending priority date or an available visa number does not wait. Consult an attorney about your timeline.
  • F-1 and other nonimmigrant visa holders with employment-based sponsorship in progress should immediately discuss the implications with their immigration counsel.

Frequently Asked Questions

What did USCIS change with PM-602-0199?

USCIS issued policy memo PM-602-0199 in May 2026, directing officers to apply broader discretionary judgment when adjudicating adjustment of status (Form I-485) applications. Officers may now consider factors beyond whether the applicant is technically eligible — including criminal history, prior immigration violations, and other adverse factors — when deciding whether to approve an application.

Does this affect pending I-485 cases?

Yes. There is no grandfathering provision protecting cases already pending at USCIS. Officers may apply the new discretionary standard to applications that were filed before the memo was issued. Applicants with pending I-485 cases and any adverse factors in their background should consult an immigration attorney promptly.

Should spouses of U.S. citizens be worried?

Immediate relatives of U.S. citizens — including spouses, minor children, and parents — have the strongest eligibility basis for adjustment of status and are generally at lower risk from this policy change, provided they have a clean record and no prior immigration violations. If you are the spouse of a U.S. citizen with no adverse history, this change is unlikely to affect your case — but confirming that assessment with an attorney is still the prudent step.

What about F-1 students with a pending I-485?

F-1 students and others in nonimmigrant status who are adjusting through employment-based or other non-immediate-relative categories may face higher scrutiny under the new discretionary standard, especially if they have overstays, gaps in authorized status, or other compliance issues in their immigration history. If you are an F-1 student with a pending I-485, contact an immigration attorney immediately to assess your specific case.

Should I withdraw my pending I-485?

Do not withdraw a pending I-485 without first consulting an immigration attorney. Withdrawing forfeits your priority date and any pending benefits associated with the application and may make your situation worse, not better. An attorney can assess the actual risk in your specific case before any action is taken.

Is the "extraordinary circumstances" language actually in the memo?

No. The phrase "extraordinary circumstances" appeared in the USCIS press release accompanying the policy memo — not in the text of PM-602-0199 itself. The memo directs officers to exercise broader discretion but does not use that phrase as a legal standard or threshold. Headlines describing the change as requiring applicants to prove extraordinary circumstances overstated what the memo's text requires.
